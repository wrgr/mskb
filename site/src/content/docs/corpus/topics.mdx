The MSKB corpus covers **18 research topics** (17 numbered topics plus TOPIC-1b — Natural History) organized in three reading layers. The baseline is 2 T1 seeds per topic, with targeted seed augmentation where citation-structure coverage is thin (TOPIC-09, TOPIC-13) or where a third anchor redistributed from a retired topic improves coverage (TOPIC-08).

Read order: Foundation → Clinical → Context/Future. You can enter at any layer but the epistemological dependencies flow in this direction.

<Aside type="note" title="TOPIC-## codes vs tier codes (T1–T4)">
MSKB uses two separate code systems that previously shared the "T" prefix — now disambiguated:

- **TOPIC-## codes** (this page) — the primary corpus taxonomy used for selection and balance. TOPIC-00 through TOPIC-15, plus TOPIC-1b.
- **Tier codes T1–T4** — corpus tiers by selection mechanism (T1 seeds, T2 cross-seed connected, T3 velocity/emerging, T4 expert signal).

The other classification system — **Leiden citation clusters** (9 clusters, bibliometric) — is available in the [Citation Topics](/mskb/topics/) sidebar and `explorer_graph.json` as an alternative view; not used in selection or balance.

See [Methodology](/mskb/corpus/methodology/#classification-systems) for a full explanation.
</Aside>

## Foundation layer

Topics that establish the biological vocabulary and disease framing prerequisite to all subsequent topics.

### TOPIC-00 — Epidemiology & Global Burden <Badge text="Foundation" variant="note" />

**Why it matters:** Quantitative global frame; geographic diversity; disease scale worldwide.

The global burden framing establishes scale (2.8 million people with MS worldwide), geographic distribution, incidence and prevalence trends, and mortality patterns. It is the context within which all clinical and mechanistic topics operate and provides the baseline against which intervention effects are measured at population level.

### TOPIC-1b — Natural History <Badge text="Foundation" variant="note" />

**Why it matters:** Pre-DMT disease trajectory; disability progression independent of relapses; baseline for treatment effect measurement.

The Lyon cohort (Confavreux et al. 2000, *NEJM*) established that disability progression, once underway, proceeds independently of prior or concurrent relapse activity. This finding is the conceptual foundation for the entire progressive MS and smoldering disease research agenda — it directly challenged the assumption that suppressing relapses would prevent long-term disability.

TOPIC-1b is numbered 1b (not a new integer) for backward compatibility with earlier versions of the orientation guide. Post-rerun monitoring: verify that TOPIC-1b generates a distinct corpus from TOPIC-08 — the Lyon cohort citation neighborhood overlaps substantially with progressive MS literature.

### TOPIC-02 — Pathophysiology <Badge text="Foundation" variant="note" />

**Why it matters:** Demyelination vs neurodegeneration duality; lesion biology; why peripheral immune drugs fail in progressive MS.

The immune-vs-neurodegeneration framing (Trapp & Nave 2008) explains why treatments that suppress peripheral inflammation can control relapses but fail to prevent disability progression. Understanding lesion heterogeneity (Lassmann 2018) explains why imaging correlates imperfectly with disability.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Seeds** | MS-T1-005 (Lassmann 2018, *CSHP Med*) · MS-T1-006 (Trapp & Nave 2008, *Ann Rev Neurosci*) |
| **Target** | 25–30 documents |
| **Primary doc types** | Mechanistic Review |

### TOPIC-03 — Genetics <Badge text="Foundation" variant="note" />

**Why it matters:** HLA architecture; 200+ GWAS loci; polygenic risk; why MS is an immune-dysregulation disease.

The IMSGC GWAS (2019) identified 233 risk variants and established that MS is genetically an immune-dysregulation disease — the majority of non-HLA loci are expressed in immune cells. This underpins the rationale for immunotherapy as the primary therapeutic strategy.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Seeds** | MS-T1-007 (IMSGC GWAS 2019, *Science*) · MS-T1-008 (Patsopoulos 2018, *CSHP Med*) |
| **Target** | 20–25 documents |
| **Primary doc types** | Epidemiological/Burden · Mechanistic Review |

---

### TOPIC-04 — Risk Factors & Etiology <Badge text="Foundation" variant="note" />

**Why it matters:** EBV; vitamin D; smoking; latitude; multi-factor model; emerging vs settled science.

The Bjornevik 2022 *Science* paper (10 million US military serum samples) established a near-causal relationship between EBV infection and subsequent MS. This is the most significant etiological finding in recent MS research and reshapes how the field thinks about prevention.

|---|---|

| **Target** | 20–25 documents |
| **Primary doc types** | Epidemiological/Burden · Mechanistic Review |

## Clinical layer

Topics presupposing the Foundation vocabulary — diagnosis, treatment, and management.

### TOPIC-05 — Diagnosis & Monitoring <Badge text="Clinical" variant="tip" />

**Why it matters:** MRI criteria; gadolinium; PRL; central vein sign; evolving McDonald criteria.

MRI is the primary diagnostic and monitoring tool in MS. The MAGNIMS-CMSC-NAIMS consensus (2021) governs MRI monitoring in clinical practice. The Absinta 2019 paramagnetic rim lesion (PRL) paper connects imaging to the smoldering disease mechanism and bridges TOPIC-05 and TOPIC-08.

| **Target** | 25–30 documents |
| **Primary doc types** | Consensus/Guideline · Epidemiological/Burden |

### TOPIC-06 — Biomarkers <Badge text="Clinical" variant="tip" />

**Why it matters:** NfL; GFAP; CHI3L1; serum vs CSF; treatment monitoring; prognostic use.

Neurofilament light chain (NfL) is the first blood biomarker to enter routine MS clinical practice for treatment monitoring. The Khalil 2018 *Nat Rev Neurology* review is cited by virtually every subsequent NfL paper and generates the largest forward citation neighborhood of any seed in this topic. Both seeds are NfL-focused — the framing anchor R4 (Lancet Neurology 2025 biomarker review) provides expansion coverage for GFAP, CHI3L1, and multi-analyte panels.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Seeds** | MS-T1-013 (Khalil et al. 2018 NfL, *Nat Rev Neurol*) · MS-T1-014 (Bittner et al. 2021 serum NfL, *Brain*) |
| **Target** | 25–30 documents |
| **Primary doc types** | Mechanistic Review |

### TOPIC-07 — Disease-Modifying Therapies <Badge text="Clinical" variant="tip" />

**Why it matters:** DMT classes; escalation vs treat-to-target; B-cell depletion paradigm; progressive MS treatment.

The OPERA (RRMS) and ORATORIO (PPMS) trials established ocrelizumab as the standard of care for both relapsing and primary progressive MS, cementing the B-cell depletion paradigm. Both seeds are 2017 ocrelizumab trials from the same program — expansion bias toward B-cell depletion literature is expected. The escalation vs. treat-to-target debate, natalizumab, alemtuzumab, BTK inhibitor, and SPMS treatment neighborhoods are expected to appear via T2 expansion from R1/R2 framing anchors; verify coverage after next pipeline rerun.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Seeds** | MS-T1-015 (Hauser et al. 2017 OPERA, *NEJM*) · MS-T1-016 (Montalban et al. 2017 ORATORIO, *NEJM*) |
| **Target** | 30–35 documents |
| **Primary doc types** | Landmark RCT |

### TOPIC-08 — Progressive MS & Smoldering <Badge text="Clinical" variant="tip" />

**Why it matters:** PIRA; smoldering inflammation; why relapse suppression ≠ disability prevention; biggest unmet need.

Progression independent of relapse activity (PIRA) is the primary driver of long-term disability in MS. The smoldering MS framework (Giovannoni et al. 2022) argues that chronic, low-grade CNS inflammation persists even when peripheral inflammation is suppressed — explaining why highly effective DMTs fail to fully prevent disability accumulation. Ontaneda et al. 2016 (*Lancet*) provides the research-agenda bridge across progressive disease biology, trial strategy, and repair — redistributed here from retired TOPIC-16 in v1.3.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Seeds** | MS-T1-017 (Lublin et al. 2014 phenotype definitions, *Neurology*) · MS-T1-018 (Giovannoni et al. 2022 smoldering MS, *TAND*) · MS-T1-040 (Ontaneda et al. 2016 progressive MS prospects, *Lancet*) |
| **Target** | 25–30 documents |
| **Primary doc types** | Consensus/Guideline · Mechanistic Review |

### TOPIC-09 — Patient-Reported Outcomes <Badge text="Clinical" variant="tip" />

**Why it matters:** EDSS limitations; fatigue; cognition; PRO instruments; trial endpoint gaps; digital health.

EDSS is the primary disability scale in MS but is insensitive to fatigue, cognition, and upper extremity function — domains that matter most to patients. This topic covers the PRO instrument landscape and the growing literature on composite endpoints and digital monitoring.

### TOPIC-10 — Comorbidities <Badge text="Clinical" variant="tip" />

**Why it matters:** Depression; anxiety; cognitive impairment; bladder dysfunction; interaction with disability.

Psychiatric comorbidity affects ~50% of people with MS and amplifies disability, reduces quality of life, and complicates treatment decisions. The Marrie 2015 systematic review established the epidemiological baseline for comorbidity burden; Chiaravalloti & DeLuca 2008 remains the foundational cognitive neuroscience reference.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Seeds** | MS-T1-021 (Marrie et al. 2015 psychiatric comorbidity, *MSJ*) · MS-T1-022 (Chiaravalloti & DeLuca 2008 cognition, *Lancet Neurol*) |
| **Target** | 20–25 documents |
| **Primary doc types** | Systematic Review/Meta-Analysis · Mechanistic Review |

### TOPIC-11 — Pregnancy & MS <Badge text="Clinical" variant="tip" />

**Why it matters:** Relapse-rebound risk; DMT teratogenicity; washout; post-partum; breastfeeding.

The PRIMS cohort (Confavreux et al. 1998, *NEJM*) established the pregnancy relapse trajectory — protective during the third trimester, rebound risk post-partum — cited by every subsequent pregnancy/MS paper. The Krysko 2023 consensus provides the current clinical guidance on DMT use in pregnancy.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Seeds** | MS-T1-023 (Confavreux et al. 1998 PRIMS, *NEJM*) · MS-T1-024 (Krysko et al. 2023 family planning, *Lancet Neurol*) |
| **Target** | 15–20 documents |
| **Primary doc types** | Natural History/Registry · Consensus/Guideline |

## Context/Future layer

Topics most meaningful after establishing the Clinical baseline.

### TOPIC-12 — Pediatric MS <Badge text="Context/Future" variant="caution" />

**Why it matters:** ADEM differential; neurodevelopmental stakes; IPMSSG criteria; pediatric EBV data.

MS in children and adolescents has distinct diagnostic challenges (differential from ADEM and other demyelinating diseases), higher relapse rates, and neurodevelopmental stakes not present in adult disease. The PARADIGMS trial (Chitnis et al. 2018, *NEJM*) is the only head-to-head DMT RCT in a pediatric MS population.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Seeds** | MS-T1-025 (Krupp et al. 2013 IPMSSG criteria, *MSJ*) · MS-T1-026 (Chitnis et al. 2018 PARADIGMS, *NEJM*) |
| **Target** | 20–25 documents |
| **Primary doc types** | Consensus/Guideline · Landmark RCT |

### TOPIC-13 — Equity & SDOH <Badge text="Context/Future" variant="caution" />

**Why it matters:** Structural racism; Black/Hispanic disparities; diagnostic delay; access to high-efficacy DMTs.

Equity research is placed in the Context/Future layer as a reading recommendation, not a value judgment. Its critical function — interrogating assumptions embedded in the clinical literature — is most powerful when read against the Clinical baseline it critiques.

TOPIC-13 uses four complementary seeds spanning prevalence (Wallin 2023, Langer-Gould 2022), incidence (Langer-Gould 2013), and SES-severity outcomes (Gray-Roncal 2021). The dedicated review anchor R5 (Amezcua et al. 2021) remains the bridge for structurally disconnected equity literature.

### TOPIC-14 — Clinical AI & Learning Systems <Badge text="Context/Future" variant="caution" />

**Why it matters:** Lesion segmentation; progression prediction; treatment recommendation; implementation gap; explainability.

Clinical AI in MS spans lesion segmentation (mature, deployed), progression prediction (active research), and treatment recommendation (early stage). The implementation gap — between what AI can do in controlled settings and what is deployed in clinical practice — is the primary open problem.

### TOPIC-15 — Emerging Frontiers <Badge text="Context/Future" variant="caution" />

**Why it matters:** Data-driven reclassification; remyelination; neuroprotection; EBV vaccine prevention.

The Louapre 2025 *Nature Medicine* paper exemplifies the emerging frontier: data-driven reclassification of MS that challenges the traditional phenotype taxonomy using large-scale longitudinal data. The Franklin & Ffrench-Constant 2008 remyelination review provides the mechanistic foundation for therapeutic remyelination programs in clinical development.

Note: these two seeds have structurally distinct citation neighborhoods (AI/ML methods vs. remyelination biology). Review corpus composition post-rerun — if clusters are non-overlapping, consider splitting or folding remyelination into TOPIC-08.
