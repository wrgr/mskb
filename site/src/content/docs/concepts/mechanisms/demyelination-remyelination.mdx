Demyelination is the loss of the myelin sheath that insulates CNS axons. In MS, inflammatory demyelination is driven by adaptive immunity (T cells, B cells, antibodies) and amplified by innate cells (microglia, macrophages). Oligodendrocytes — the myelin-producing cells of the CNS — are killed directly or lose the ability to maintain their sheaths. Remyelination is possible via oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs), but fails increasingly with disease progression, leaving axons exposed and vulnerable to transection.

## Key questions

- What exactly happens at the axon when oligodendrocytes are damaged or killed?
- Why do OPCs fail to remyelinate older, chronic MS lesions?
- Is progressive disability driven more by ongoing demyelination, or by cumulative axonal loss?
