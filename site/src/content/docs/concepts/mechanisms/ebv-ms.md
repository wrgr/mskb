## Learning objectives

- Summarise the prospective epidemiological evidence linking EBV infection to subsequent MS onset
- Explain the molecular mimicry hypothesis: how EBNA1 antibodies cross-react with GlialCAM
- Distinguish the roles of EBV infection timing, HLA genotype, and immune state in MS risk
- Discuss the implications of EBV causality for MS prevention via vaccination

## Key questions

- Why does virtually everyone with MS have prior EBV infection, yet most EBV-infected people never develop MS?
- How long after EBV infection does MS typically emerge, and what does the latency imply about mechanism?
- What is the evidence that EBNA1 mimics GlialCAM, and how strong is the molecular mimicry case?

## Core idea

Björnevik et al. (2022, *Science*) followed 10 million US military personnel with serial serum samples. Among 955 who developed MS, every single one had prior EBV infection; risk increased 32-fold after EBV seroconversion. This established EBV as a near-necessary cause. Lanz et al. (2022, *Nature*) then showed that EBV EBNA1 shares structural homology with GlialCAM, an astrocyte and oligodendrocyte protein — anti-EBNA1 antibodies cross-react with CNS tissue in a proportion of MS patients, providing a molecular mechanism for the long latency. Together these findings place EBV at the centre of the MS prevention agenda and support ongoing EBV vaccine development (mRNA-1189, Phase 2) as a potential MS prevention strategy.
