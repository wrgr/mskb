Nearly every person with MS has been infected with Epstein–Barr virus (EBV), but most EBV-infected people never develop MS. A 20-year prospective cohort of US military personnel found that EBV seroconversion was associated with a 32-fold increase in MS risk, the strongest known environmental risk factor. Mechanistically, molecular mimicry between the EBV nuclear antigen EBNA1 and the CNS adhesion molecule GlialCAM has been demonstrated in clonally expanded MS B cells, offering a plausible link from an old viral infection to chronic CNS autoimmunity.

## Key questions

- Why do nearly all MS patients have prior EBV, but not all EBV-exposed people get MS?
- How could an old infection trigger MS years later?
- Is EBV a cause of MS, or a correlate of something else we haven't measured?
- Should an EBV vaccine change the MS prevention calculus?
