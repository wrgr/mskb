## Learning objectives

- Explain what happens at the cellular level when an MS lesion forms
- Describe oligodendrocyte injury and the limits of remyelination
- Distinguish acute inflammatory demyelination from chronic progressive neurodegeneration
- Interpret why cortical (gray matter) lesions matter clinically

## Prerequisites

- [Immune System Basics for MS](/mskb/concepts/foundations/immune-system-basics/)

## Key questions

- Can the brain repair demyelinated axons? Under what conditions?
- Why is remyelination incomplete in chronic MS lesions?
- What is the difference between a T2 lesion and a "black hole"?

## Core idea

Demyelination begins when autoreactive lymphocytes and macrophages invade CNS white matter, stripping myelin from axons. Mature oligodendrocytes die; oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs) are recruited but fail to fully remyelinate in chronic lesions due to inhibitory signals (LINGO-1, PSA-NCAM) and OPC exhaustion. Demyelinated axons are metabolically vulnerable — prolonged demyelination leads to irreversible axonal transection and the clinical disability that accumulates even between relapses.
