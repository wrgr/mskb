## Learning objectives

- Describe how peripheral T cells and B cells are activated against CNS antigens
- Explain the sequence of events from BBB breach to acute inflammatory lesion formation
- Distinguish acute peripheral neuroinflammation (relapse-associated) from compartmentalized chronic neuroinflammation (smoldering/progressive)
- Connect specific inflammatory cell types to their therapeutic targets

## Key questions

- What is the initial antigen trigger that activates MS-specific T cells in the periphery?
- Why does inflammation persist behind the blood-brain barrier even when peripheral disease is controlled?
- How do microglia differ from peripheral macrophages in their role in MS pathology?

## Core idea

MS neuroinflammation operates in two spatial compartments. **Peripheral inflammation**: autoreactive CD4+ T helper cells (Th1, Th17) and CD8+ cytotoxic T cells are activated in lymph nodes, breach the blood-brain barrier via VLA-4/VCAM-1 binding, and initiate acute lesions — the target of most approved DMTs. **Compartmentalized inflammation**: within chronic MS lesions, resident microglia and astrocytes sustain smoldering activity independently of peripheral immune surveillance. Meningeal B-cell follicle-like aggregates contribute to cortical lesions and progressive neurodegeneration. This dual-compartment model explains why high-efficacy peripheral immunotherapy prevents relapses but incompletely controls progressive disability — and why CNS-penetrant agents (BTK inhibitors) are the current frontier.
