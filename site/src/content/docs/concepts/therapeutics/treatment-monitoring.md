## Learning objectives

- Define "no evidence of disease activity" (NEDA-3 and NEDA-4) as a treatment target
- Describe how serum NfL is used to detect subclinical disease activity and inform switches
- Explain the criteria for treatment failure and switching (relapses on therapy, MRI breakthrough, disability worsening)
- Discuss extended interval dosing (EID) for natalizumab and the JC virus risk calculus

## Key questions

- How much MRI activity is acceptable on a given DMT before switching?
- Can NfL replace MRI for monitoring?
- What are the JC antibody index thresholds for natalizumab extended-interval dosing?

## Core idea

The treat-to-target framework uses NEDA (No Evidence of Disease Activity) as a treatment goal: no relapses, no new T2 lesions, no disability worsening. NEDA-4 adds brain atrophy rate (<0.4%/year). Serum NfL is increasingly used as an early warning signal — elevated NfL predicts subsequent MRI activity 6–12 months ahead. Treatment switches are indicated by clinical relapses on therapy, ≥2 new T2 lesions per year, or gadolinium-enhancing lesions after adequate treatment duration. Extended-interval natalizumab dosing reduces PML risk while largely preserving efficacy.
