## Learning objectives

- Categorise approved DMTs by mechanism (immunomodulatory vs. immunosuppressive vs. selective trafficking vs. B-cell depletion)
- State relative efficacy of first-line, second-line, and high-efficacy agents using ARR and disability endpoints
- Describe the "treat-to-target" and "early high-efficacy" treatment paradigms
- Identify which phenotypes have approved DMTs (RRMS, PPMS — ocrelizumab; SPMS — siponimod, cladribine)

## Key questions

- Why do interferons and glatiramer acetate have lower efficacy than B-cell depleting agents?
- What evidence supports the "early high-efficacy" strategy?
- Why are PPMS DMT options still limited?

## Core idea

More than 20 DMTs are now approved for relapsing MS, spanning from modest-efficacy platform therapies (interferon-beta, glatiramer acetate, teriflunomide, dimethyl fumarate) to high-efficacy agents (natalizumab, fingolimod, ocrelizumab, ofatumumab, cladribine, alemtuzumab). The ECTRIMS/EAN 2023 guidelines increasingly support early high-efficacy treatment rather than escalation, particularly for younger patients with active disease. Only ocrelizumab, ofatumumab (recently), and siponimod/ozanimod have demonstrated efficacy in progressive phenotypes.
