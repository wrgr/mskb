## Learning objectives

- Explain why depleting CD20+ B cells reduces MS disease activity despite MS being historically framed as a T-cell disease
- Compare the approved anti-CD20 agents by route, dosing, and pivotal trial outcomes
- Describe the safety profile: hypogammaglobulinemia, infection risk, and PML risk stratification
- Interpret the ORATORIO trial (ocrelizumab in PPMS) and its significance

## Key questions

- If MS is autoimmune, why doesn't B-cell depletion cure the disease?
- What is the clinical difference between IV ocrelizumab and SC ofatumumab?

## Core idea

Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibodies deplete mature B cells by ADCC, CDC, and apoptosis, dramatically reducing relapse rates (~50% over interferons, 80–90% over placebo). Ocrelizumab became the first approved therapy for PPMS (ORATORIO, 2017). The B-cell role in MS involves antigen presentation, cytokine production (TNF, lymphotoxin), and ectopic lymphoid follicle formation in progressive disease — not primarily antibody secretion. Long-term B-cell depletion leads to progressive hypogammaglobulinemia, requiring monitoring of IgG levels.
