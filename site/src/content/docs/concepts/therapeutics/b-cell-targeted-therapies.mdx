The recognition that B cells — not just T cells — drive MS pathology was a turning point in the therapeutic landscape. Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibodies (rituximab, ocrelizumab, ofatumumab, ublituximab) deplete circulating CD20+ B cells and are highly effective at reducing relapses and new MRI lesions in relapsing MS, and ocrelizumab is also approved for primary progressive MS. Understanding when, why, and for how long to treat — and the infection and immunoglobulin risks of chronic depletion — is a core therapeutic concept.

## Key questions

- Why target B cells instead of T cells, when CD4+ T cells were long thought to be central?
- How completely do anti-CD20 drugs deplete B cells, and what happens to plasma cells and immunoglobulin levels?
- What are the infection risks, and how should you monitor for and mitigate them?
- What's the case for extended-interval dosing, and what are the trade-offs?
