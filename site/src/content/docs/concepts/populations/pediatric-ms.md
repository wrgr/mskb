## Learning objectives

- Apply IPMSSG (International Pediatric MS Study Group) diagnostic criteria
- Distinguish pediatric MS from ADEM (acute disseminated encephalomyelitis) and MOG antibody disease
- Describe the epidemiology of pediatric MS (2–5% of all MS; mean onset age 12–15)
- Explain the neurodevelopmental consequences (educational, cognitive, psychosocial) of MS onset in childhood

## Key questions

- Why does ADEM look like MS on MRI — and how do you differentiate them?
- What does EBV timing in pediatric MS tell us about disease etiology?
- Are the same DMTs used in adults appropriate for children?

## Core idea

Pediatric MS represents 2–5% of all MS cases. Diagnosis is complicated by a higher rate of ADEM-like presentations (larger, more enhancing lesions; encephalopathy), and MOG antibody disease must be excluded. The IPMSSG 2012 (revised) criteria adapt McDonald requirements for age. Early-onset MS has higher relapse rates but may have paradoxically better initial recovery. However, the brain is still developing — pediatric-onset MS causes white matter volume loss, cortical thinning, and cognitive impairment in a still-maturing brain, with consequences for academic achievement and psychosocial development. Data on pediatric EBV-MS timing (Bjornevik 2022) directly support the causative role of primary EBV infection during childhood.

## DMT considerations in children

- Interferon-beta and glatiramer acetate are licensed for pediatric use (≥10-12 years in some jurisdictions)
- Natalizumab and fingolimod have paediatric RCT data (PARADIGMS trial)
- Ocrelizumab off-label use is increasing but formal paediatric trial data are limited
