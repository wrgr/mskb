## Learning objectives

- State the current female:male ratio (~3:1) and describe the temporal trend and possible explanations
- Explain how oestrogen and progesterone modulate T-cell function and MS relapse risk
- Describe sex differences in disease course (women: more relapses; men: faster disability progression)
- Distinguish biological sex from gender identity and discuss implications for MS clinical care

## Key questions

- Why has the female:male ratio shifted over time — changes in smoking, vitamin D, or immune-regulatory evolution?
- How do oestrogens explain the relapse reduction in pregnancy (third trimester)?
- Are there sex-specific differences in DMT response?

## Core idea

MS disproportionately affects women (3:1 ratio), yet disability worsens faster in men and PPMS is more common in men. The rising female:male ratio over 50 years mirrors trends in other autoimmune diseases and is likely explained by changing environmental exposures (smoking rates in women, vitamin D, microbiome). Sex hormones profoundly modulate disease activity: the third-trimester relapse reduction is mediated by oestriol-driven Th2 immune shift; estriol supplementation trials showed modest benefit. Testosterone in men may partially explain milder relapsing disease but more rapid neurodegeneration. Gender identity is an emerging area: transgender pwMS face healthcare barriers and uncertain effects of gender-affirming hormone therapy on disease activity.
