## Learning objectives

- Describe racial and ethnic disparities in MS prevalence, phenotype severity, and diagnostic delay
- Explain how SDOH (income, education, insurance, geography) affect access to neurologists and high-efficacy DMTs
- Identify the historical basis for the underrepresentation of Black and Hispanic patients in MS clinical trials
- Discuss how structural racism and implicit bias operate in MS clinical practice

## Prerequisites

- [MS Epidemiology](/mskb/concepts/foundations/ms-epidemiology/)
- [DMT Overview](/mskb/concepts/therapeutics/dmt-overview/)

## Key questions

- Why do Black Americans have more severe MS and worse outcomes despite similar diagnostic criteria?
- What is the evidence for diagnostic delay in non-white patients?
- How does the cost of high-efficacy DMTs interact with race, insurance, and geography to produce disparate outcomes?

## Core idea

MS was historically described as a disease of white Northern European populations, but MS affects all racial/ethnic groups. Black Americans have higher relapse rates, faster disability progression, and greater cognitive impairment than white Americans despite similar disease duration — attributable to both biological differences (immune gene expression, comorbidity burden) and structural factors (diagnostic delay, later access to neurologists, lower rates of high-efficacy DMT prescription). Hispanic Americans are the fastest-growing MS population in the US. Geographic inequity — "MS deserts" in rural areas — compounds access barriers. Clinical trial populations historically overrepresent white, educated, high-SES participants, limiting generalisability. JAMA Neurology 2021 (Amezcua et al.) is the field's review anchor for health disparities.

## Patient perspective

Patients from marginalised communities report experiences of being dismissed, having symptoms attributed to anxiety, and receiving less aggressive treatment recommendations. The MS community (NMSS, iConquerMS, Can Do MS) has launched equity-focused initiatives. Patient researchers and community health workers are increasingly co-investigators in MS equity research.
