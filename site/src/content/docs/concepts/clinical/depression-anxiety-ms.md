## Learning objectives

- State lifetime prevalence of depression (~50%) and anxiety (~30–50%) in MS
- Distinguish biological depression (directly caused by CNS pathology) from reactive depression (adjustment to chronic illness)
- Describe the bidirectional relationship: depression worsens fatigue, cognition, and DMT adherence; neuroinflammation drives depression biology
- Outline evidence-based treatment including SSRIs, CBT, exercise, and MS-specific adaptations

## Key questions

- How much of MS depression is biological versus reactive — and does the distinction matter for treatment?
- Does depression directly accelerate neurodegeneration, or is it a marker of more aggressive disease?
- Why are depression and anxiety consistently under-identified in MS clinic encounters?

## Core idea

Depression affects approximately 50% of pwMS over the disease course — higher than any other neurological disease — and 2–3× higher than the general population. Mechanisms include direct inflammatory pathology (cytokine-driven neuroinflammatory depression), lesion location (prefrontal cortex, limbic connections), and reactive adjustment to disability and uncertainty. Depression is the strongest predictor of quality of life in MS, stronger than EDSS or lesion burden. The JLA identified psychological health as the #4 research priority; animal and human data show that depression may amplify neuroinflammation bidirectionally. SSRIs have RCT evidence; CBT adapted for MS shows efficacy; exercise benefits both mood and fatigue simultaneously.

## Patient perspective

Surveys consistently show that mental health care is the most frequently unmet clinical need among pwMS. Patients report stigma, clinician dismissal ("it's normal to be sad"), and fragmented care between neurology and psychiatry. The PROMS initiative identifies social wellbeing and emotional functioning among the 28 top patient-priority domains. Online CBT and self-management programmes have growing evidence for reducing barriers.
