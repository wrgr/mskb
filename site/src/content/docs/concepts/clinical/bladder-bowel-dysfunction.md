## Learning objectives

- State prevalence of bladder dysfunction (>80%) and bowel dysfunction (>50%) in MS
- Describe the neuroanatomical basis of neurogenic bladder in MS (pontine micturition centre, spinal cord lesions)
- Distinguish storage symptoms (urgency, frequency, nocturia) from voiding symptoms (hesitancy, incomplete emptying)
- Outline an evidence-based assessment and treatment pathway (urodynamics, anticholinergics, mirabegron, intermittent catheterisation)

## Key questions

- Why do upper motor neuron lesions (cervical/thoracic cord) cause overactive bladder rather than urinary retention?
- How does urinary tract infection interact with MS disease activity (Uhthoff phenomenon)?
- What are the barriers to patients reporting bladder/bowel symptoms?

## Core idea

Bladder dysfunction affects >80% of pwMS during the disease course and is directly caused by demyelination in the spinal cord disrupting the pontine-sacral micturition pathway. Overactive bladder (urgency, frequency, nocturia) is most common and responds to anticholinergics (oxybutynin, solifenacin) or mirabegron (β3-agonist); urinary retention is less common but requires intermittent self-catheterisation. Bowel dysfunction (constipation most common; urgency/incontinence less so) is closely linked to pelvic floor dysfunction and responds to fibre, laxatives, and bowel management programmes. Urinary tract infections can trigger pseudorelapses via Uhthoff phenomenon. These symptoms are under-reported due to patient embarrassment and under-screened in practice.

## Patient perspective

Bladder and bowel dysfunction are among the most embarrassing and socially limiting MS symptoms. Patients consistently report that clinicians fail to ask about them proactively. The impact on employment, relationships, and mental health is substantial. MSIF and NMSS recommend routine screening using validated tools (e.g., MSISQ-19, NBD score) at every clinical encounter.
