## Learning objectives

- Summarise RCT evidence for aerobic exercise on fatigue, cognition, depression, and physical function in MS

- Identify barriers to exercise uptake in pwMS and evidence-based strategies to overcome them

## Key questions

- Is exercise neuroprotective in MS, or does it only symptomatically benefit patients?
- How should exercise prescription be adapted for pwMS with significant spasticity or balance impairment?
- What is the evidence for multidisciplinary inpatient rehabilitation vs. outpatient/home-based models?

## Core idea

Exercise and rehabilitation are now recommended as first-line adjunctive management across all MS phenotypes. Systematic reviews (Cochrane, 2024) confirm that aerobic exercise improves fatigue (MFIS), walking speed (25-foot walk), and mood. Resistance training preserves muscle function in progressive MS. Emerging animal evidence suggests exercise increases BDNF, promotes remyelination, and reduces CNS inflammation — but human neuroprotection data remain limited. The JLA identified exercise as its 5th-highest priority, outranking most pharmacological interventions. Telerehabilitation has comparable efficacy to in-person therapy for many outcomes.

## Multidisciplinary care

The JLA #3 priority — "Does multidisciplinary care improve outcomes?" — directly connects to rehabilitation. MS specialist nurses, physiotherapists, occupational therapists, speech and language therapists, neuropsychologists, and social workers each contribute to functional outcomes that drugs alone do not address.

## Patient perspective

Patients frequently report being told "just rest" and receiving no exercise prescription. Exercise improves self-efficacy and sense of agency — critically important for people living with an unpredictable condition. Heat sensitivity (Uhthoff phenomenon) requires cool environments but is not a contraindication to exercise.
