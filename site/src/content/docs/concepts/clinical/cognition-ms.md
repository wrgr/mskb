## Learning objectives

- State the prevalence and most common cognitive domains affected in MS (processing speed, episodic memory, executive function)
- Describe the neuroimaging correlates of MS cognitive impairment (white matter lesions, gray matter atrophy, thalamic volume)
- Identify validated cognitive screening tools used in clinical practice (SDMT, BICAMS)
- Summarise evidence for cognitive rehabilitation and disease-modifying effects on cognition

## Key questions

- Which cognitive domains are most commonly affected and why?
- Does processing speed predict disability better than EDSS?
- Is there evidence that DMTs protect against cognitive decline?

## Core idea

Cognitive impairment affects 40–70% of MS patients across all phenotypes and often precedes physical disability. Processing speed (Symbol Digit Modalities Test) is the most sensitive marker. Thalamic atrophy and cortical gray matter loss correlate strongly with cognitive dysfunction, more so than white matter lesion volume. Cognitive reserve (education, mental engagement) partially buffers decline. Moderate-quality evidence supports cognitive rehabilitation; high-efficacy DMTs may modestly preserve cognition compared to platform therapies.
