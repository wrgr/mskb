- Define shared decision-making (SDM) and distinguish it from informed consent and patient-centred care broadly
- Apply SDM frameworks (Option Grid, Ottawa Decision Aids) to the MS DMT choice scenario

- Explain the role of MS specialist nurses and patient navigators in SDM

## Key questions

- How does a clinician present relapse rate reduction (55% vs. 30%) to a patient without medical training?
- What role should fear of side effects play relative to long-term disability outcomes?
- How are patient priorities for fatigue, pregnancy plans, and employment incorporated into DMT selection?

## Core idea

Shared decision-making (SDM) in MS involves clinicians and patients jointly deliberating treatment choices using the best available evidence while integrating patient values, preferences, and lived experience. The MS context is particularly suited to SDM because: (1) there are >20 approved DMTs with different efficacy/safety/route trade-offs; (2) there is no single correct choice; (3) factors invisible to clinicians (injection phobia, pregnancy plans, fear of progressive MS, work schedule) are decisive. Decision aids (Ottawa DA, MSBase SDM toolkit) improve decision quality and reduce decisional regret. The JLA ranked multidisciplinary care 3rd overall — SDM is the clinical mechanism that operationalises this.

## Patient perspective

Patient advocacy organisations (MSIF, MS Society UK, NMSS) have published position statements calling for every DMT discussion to use SDM principles. Research shows clinicians consistently overestimate how much they involve patients in decisions. The rise of patient-reported outcomes and digital health apps is shifting power toward patient agency in monitoring and treatment adjustments.
