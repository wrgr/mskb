## Learning objectives

- Define paramagnetic rim lesions (PRLs) and explain the MRI sequences used to detect them
- Describe the pathological substrate of PRLs (iron-laden microglia and macrophages at the lesion edge)
- Explain the relationship between PRL count and disability progression in MS
- Discuss the role of PRLs as trial endpoints and in the 2024 McDonald criteria revision

## Key questions

- What makes a lesion "paramagnetic" and why does the rim appear on susceptibility-weighted sequences?
- Why do PRLs persist for years while most acute MS lesions eventually become inactive?
- How was the central vein sign incorporated into the 2024 McDonald criteria, and how does this relate to PRLs?

## Core idea

Paramagnetic rim lesions (PRLs) are a subtype of chronic active MS lesion in which activated microglia and macrophages laden with iron (hemosiderin) form a metabolically active rim at the lesion edge. On susceptibility-weighted MRI (Phase, QSM, SWI), this rim appears as a dark ring surrounding the lesion core. PRLs persist for years, slowly expanding outward (slowly expanding lesions, SELs), and their count correlates strongly with faster disability accumulation, brain atrophy, and conversion to progressive disease. Inclusion of the central vein sign — a related iron-based imaging feature of perivenular MS lesions — in the 2024 McDonald criteria revision reflects the growing clinical relevance of susceptibility-weighted MS imaging. PRL count is now a candidate surrogate endpoint in Phase 2 trials of agents targeting smoldering inflammation, including BTK inhibitors.
