## Learning objectives

- Distinguish T2-hyperintense, T1-hypointense ("black holes"), and gadolinium-enhancing lesions and their clinical significance
- Identify characteristic MS lesion locations (periventricular, juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord)
- Explain the central vein sign and paramagnetic rim lesions as imaging biomarkers of MS pathology
- Interpret MRI in the context of the McDonald DIS and DIT criteria

## Key questions

- What is a Dawson's finger and why does it occur periventricularly?
- When does gadolinium enhancement occur and what does it indicate about lesion age?
- What is the central vein sign and how does it distinguish MS lesions from mimics?

## Core idea

MS lesions show a predilection for periventricular, juxtacortical, infratentorial, and spinal cord locations (MAGNIMS criteria). T2/FLAIR hyperintensity marks edema and demyelination; gadolinium enhancement marks acute BBB disruption (active inflammation); T1 hypointense "black holes" mark irreversible axonal loss. The central vein sign (perilesional vein visible on 7T or susceptibility-weighted imaging) and paramagnetic rim lesions (chronic active lesions with iron-laden microglia at the periphery) improve specificity for MS versus mimics.
