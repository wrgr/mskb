## Learning objectives

- Define the four MS phenotypes and their diagnostic requirements (RRMS, PPMS, SPMS, CIS)
- Describe the radiologically isolated syndrome (RIS) and its risk of conversion
- Explain how phenotype classification guides choice of DMT
- Recognise limitations of the phenotype framework in the era of high-efficacy therapy

## Prerequisites

- [What is Multiple Sclerosis?](/mskb/concepts/foundations/what-is-ms/)

## Key questions

- What distinguishes RRMS from SPMS — and is the distinction biologically meaningful?
- What proportion of CIS patients convert to MS within 10 years?
- Should RIS be treated?

## Core idea

The 2013 Lublin/Reingold phenotype revision defines RRMS (discrete relapses with full or partial recovery), PPMS (gradual progression from onset without relapses), SPMS (initial RRMS course followed by steady progression), and CIS (first clinical episode not yet meeting McDonald criteria). Phenotype classification has implications for trial design and regulatory approval but is increasingly viewed as a simplification — underlying biology shows a spectrum of inflammatory and degenerative activity across all phenotypes.
