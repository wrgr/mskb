## Learning objectives

- Explain the roles of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells in CNS inflammation
- Describe how B cells contribute to MS beyond antibody production
- Outline antigen presentation via MHC class II and the HLA-DRB1 link
- Define "molecular mimicry" and why it is implicated in MS onset

## Prerequisites

- [What is Multiple Sclerosis?](/mskb/concepts/foundations/what-is-ms/)

## Key questions

- Why do T cells attack myelin if the immune system is supposed to protect the body?
- What role do B cells play — and why do anti-CD20 therapies work so well?
- What is central tolerance and how does it break down?

## Core idea

MS is driven by autoreactive T cells (primarily Th1/Th17) and B cells that penetrate the blood-brain barrier and target myelin antigens. B cells amplify the response through antigen presentation, cytokine production, and, to a lesser degree, myelin-specific antibodies. The strongest genetic risk factor, HLA-DRB1*15:01, directly implicates antigen presentation in disease initiation.
