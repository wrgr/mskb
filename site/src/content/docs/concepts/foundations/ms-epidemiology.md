## Learning objectives

- State global MS prevalence and the north-south latitude gradient
- Describe temporal trends in MS incidence (increasing, especially in women)
- Outline the natural history — from CIS to RRMS to progressive disease
- Identify major demographic risk factors (sex, age, ethnicity)

## Prerequisites

- [What is Multiple Sclerosis?](/mskb/concepts/foundations/what-is-ms/)

## Key questions

- Why is MS more common at higher latitudes?
- Why has the female-to-male ratio increased over the past 50 years?
- What fraction of untreated RRMS patients convert to SPMS by 25 years?

## Core idea

MS affects approximately 2.8 million people worldwide (2020 Atlas). Prevalence is highest in Northern Europe, North America, and Australasia. The 3:1 female-to-male ratio has widened since the 1950s, pointing toward environmental or hormonal changes. The natural history, if untreated, involves progressive accumulation of disability; modern DMTs have substantially altered this trajectory.
