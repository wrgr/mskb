## Learning objectives

- Define MS as a chronic autoimmune, inflammatory demyelinating disease of the CNS
- Distinguish MS from other demyelinating diseases (NMO-SD, MOG-AD, ADEM)
- Recognize the four MS phenotypes and their defining features (RRMS, SPMS, PPMS, CIS)

## Prerequisites

None — this is an entry point concept.

## Key questions

- What makes MS different from other neurological diseases?
- Why does MS affect white matter in the brain and spinal cord specifically?
- How do we classify someone as having MS versus another demyelinating disease?

## Core idea

Multiple sclerosis is a chronic, immune-mediated disease in which the body's own immune cells attack myelin sheaths around axons in the central nervous system. This causes demyelinated plaques (lesions) that disrupt nerve conduction, producing episodic or progressive neurological symptoms.

The disease is heterogeneous: most patients begin with relapsing-remitting disease (RRMS), a minority present with primary progressive disease (PPMS), and many RRMS patients transition to secondary progressive disease (SPMS) over years to decades.
