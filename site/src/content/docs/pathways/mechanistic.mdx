This pathway moves from foundational immunology through the specific mechanisms of MS — how immune cells breach the blood-brain barrier, why myelin is the target, what drives neurodegeneration, and how drug targets map onto these mechanisms. It is designed for learners whose entry point is biology: a neuroscience student, a biochemist, or a pre-PhD researcher who wants to understand mechanism before memorizing drug names.

<Aside type="tip">
Each step links to a concept page with learning objectives, prerequisites, and curated papers. The mechanistic pathway assumes basic cell biology; it does not assume prior MS knowledge.
</Aside>

## Pathway sequence

<Steps>

1. **[What is MS?](/mskb/concepts/foundations/what-is-ms/)**

   Even on a mechanistic pathway, start with the clinical picture. Knowing the four phenotypes and the relapse-progression axis gives you the target your mechanism needs to explain.

2. **[T cell & B cell biology](/mskb/concepts/foundations/immune-basics/)**

   The immune system as it relates to MS. Activation, trafficking, antigen presentation, B cell differentiation, and the adaptive immune response that underlies MS pathology.

3. **[Genetic susceptibility](/mskb/concepts/mechanisms/genetic-susceptibility/)**

   HLA-DRB1\*15:01 and the ~230 non-HLA risk loci from GWAS. Why genetics alone is insufficient (identical twin concordance ~25%), and what gene-environment interaction means in practice.

4. **[Epstein-Barr virus & molecular mimicry](/mskb/concepts/mechanisms/ebv-ms/)**

   The environmental trigger. Björnevik 2022 established EBV as a necessary (not sufficient) cause. Lanz 2022 showed the molecular mechanism: EBNA1 mimics GlialCAM. This is where genetics and environment intersect.

5. **[Neuroinflammation & BBB dysfunction](/mskb/concepts/mechanisms/neuroinflammation/)**

   How activated T cells breach the blood-brain barrier, initiate local inflammation, recruit monocytes and B cells, and drive lesion formation. Why the CNS is "immune-privileged" and what breaks that privilege.

6. **[Demyelination & remyelination](/mskb/concepts/mechanisms/demyelination-remyelination/)**

   What oligodendrocytes do, what happens when they die, and why the adult CNS can partially but incompletely repair myelin. The OPC biology behind remyelination failure.

7. **[Axonal pathology & neurodegeneration](/mskb/concepts/mechanisms/axonal-pathology/)**

   The shift from inflammatory demyelination to irreversible neurodegeneration. Why "inflammation causes demyelination causes axonal loss" is an oversimplification. Mitochondrial failure, ion channel dysregulation, and Wallerian degeneration.

8. **[Lesion heterogeneity](/mskb/concepts/mechanisms/lesion-heterogeneity/)**

   Lassmann's four pathological subtypes of MS lesions. Acute active vs. chronic active vs. inactive lesions. Cortical lesions, slowly expanding lesions, and paramagnetic rim lesions as distinct biological entities.

9. **[DMT mechanisms](/mskb/concepts/therapeutics/dmt-overview/)**

   Map drug classes onto the mechanisms you now understand. Interferons (innate immune modulation), glatiramer acetate (tolerization), natalizumab (α4-integrin block at BBB), S1P modulators (lymphocyte sequestration), anti-CD20 agents (B cell depletion), BTK inhibitors (CNS-resident microglia/B cells).

10. **[Progressive MS biology](/mskb/concepts/clinical/smoldering-ms/)**

    Why progressive MS is mechanistically distinct from relapsing MS. Compartmentalized CNS inflammation, microglial activation, smoldering disease, and why peripheral immunosuppression is insufficient.

</Steps>
