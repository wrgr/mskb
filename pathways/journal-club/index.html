Undergraduate Journal Club Track

A journal club is a standing meeting where a small group reads one paper together and argues about it. This track is designed for undergraduate chapters — students who have taken intro biology and at least one of (immunology, neuroscience, biostatistics). Each session is 60–75 minutes and assumes one paper assigned in advance.

How to use this track

Pick a presenter each week (rotate). The presenter gives a 10-minute summary using the What / Why / How / So what frame; the group then works through the discussion prompts together. Use the linked concept pages as prep, not as substitutes for the paper. End each session by picking the next week’s presenter and — optionally — a dissenting discussant who argues the opposite position.

Session 1 — How to read an MS paper

Section titled “Session 1 — How to read an MS paper”

Goal. Agree on a shared method before any paper is assigned. Calibrate the group.

Pre-reading.

  • Greenhalgh T. How to read a paper: the basics of evidence-based medicine, ch. 1–3. BMJ Books. The chapter on “getting your bearings” is the core.
  • CASP critical appraisal checklists (RCT, cohort, systematic review) — casp-uk.net. Pick one that matches next week’s paper.

Discussion prompts.

  1. What is the difference between a mechanistic claim, an association claim, and a causal claim? Where does each live in a typical MS paper?
  2. When a paper says “significant reduction in relapses,” what three questions should you ask before you believe it?
  3. Why is a paper’s Figure 1 often more important than its abstract?

Supporting resources.

  • EQUATOR Network reporting guidelines — equator-network.org. Bookmark CONSORT (trials), STROBE (observational), PRISMA (reviews).
  • National MS Society’s “Research” landing page — nationalmssociety.org/Research — for framing who funds what.

Session 2 — The diagnostic frame: McDonald criteria

Section titled “Session 2 — The diagnostic frame: McDonald criteria”

Anchor paper. Thompson AJ et al. Diagnosis of multiple sclerosis: 2017 revisions of the McDonald criteria. Lancet Neurol 2018. doi:10.1016/S1474-4422(17)30470-2

Why this matters. Before we can argue about mechanisms or drugs, we need a shared rule for who has MS. McDonald is that rule. Every downstream paper inherits its assumptions.

Discussion prompts.

  1. What do dissemination in space (DIS) and dissemination in time (DIT) mean operationally? What counts as evidence for each?
  2. How do oligoclonal bands substitute for DIT in the 2017 revision, and why was that change controversial?
  3. What kinds of patients will be misdiagnosed by these criteria? In which direction — false positives or false negatives?

MSKB concepts. McDonald criteria · MRI patterns · CSF & blood biomarkers

Supporting resources.

  • MAGNIMS consensus guidelines for MRI in MS — usually free at magnims.eu.
  • Solomon AJ et al. The contemporary spectrum of multiple sclerosis misdiagnosis. Neurology 2016. A field favourite to pair with Thompson 2018.

Session 3 — Clinical phenotypes

Section titled “Session 3 — Clinical phenotypes”

Anchor paper. Lublin FD et al. Defining the clinical course of multiple sclerosis: the 2013 revisions. Neurology 2014. doi:10.1212/WNL.0000000000000560

Why this matters. RRMS, SPMS, PPMS, CIS, RIS — these labels drive every clinical trial’s inclusion criteria. They are also less biologically clean than they look.

Discussion prompts.

  1. Which of the four phenotypes is defined by a mechanism, and which are defined by pattern of observation?
  2. If a patient has “no new lesions, no relapses, slowly worsening EDSS,” what are they called under Lublin — and does that label help or mislead?
  3. Read the phenotype definitions alongside PIRA. How does PIRA challenge the 2014 categories?

MSKB concepts. MS phenotypes · Disease course

Supporting resources.

  • Confavreux C et al. Relapses and progression of disability in multiple sclerosis. NEJM 2000. doi:10.1056/NEJM200011163432001 — the Lyon natural-history cohort that shaped the phenotype idea.

Session 4 — The immune story

Section titled “Session 4 — The immune story”

Anchor paper. Compston A, Coles A. Multiple sclerosis. Lancet 2008. doi:10.1016/S0140-6736(08)61620-7

Why this matters. This is the canonical pre-anti-CD20 synthesis of MS immunology. Reading it in 2026 is an exercise in noticing what has aged well (the role of autoreactive T cells, HLA risk) and what has aged badly (underestimation of B cells and EBV).

Discussion prompts.

  1. What evidence in 2008 pointed to a T-cell-dominant model of MS? What evidence since has reweighted the story towards B cells?
  2. How does Compston & Coles describe EBV? Compare to session 5.
  3. Pick one therapeutic claim in the paper that would be written differently today. What changed?

MSKB concepts. Immune basics · Neuroinflammation

Supporting resources.

  • Trapp BD, Nave KA. Multiple sclerosis: an immune or neurodegenerative disorder? Annu Rev Neurosci 2008 — read in parallel to hear the neurodegenerative counter-argument.

Session 5 — EBV as a necessary cause

Section titled “Session 5 — EBV as a necessary cause”

Anchor paper. Björnevik K et al. Longitudinal analysis reveals high prevalence of Epstein-Barr virus associated with multiple sclerosis. Science 2022. doi:10.1126/science.abj8222

Why this matters. 10 million US military recruits, 20 years of serial blood samples, 955 MS cases. Hazard ratio 32.4 after EBV seroconversion. This is the strongest evidence yet that MS has a near-necessary environmental cause.

Discussion prompts.

  1. Why does a longitudinal serial-sample design answer a question that a cross-sectional seroprevalence study cannot?
  2. The 955 MS cases include only one who was EBV-negative at disease onset. How should that one case be interpreted?
  3. If EBV is necessary, why isn’t it sufficient? What does the gap between ~95% of adults being EBV+ and ~0.3% having MS tell us?

MSKB concepts. EBV & MS · Environmental risk factors

Supporting resources.

  • Ascherio A, Munger KL. EBV and MS: a causal argument. Ann Neurol commentary series — solid lay-friendly framing.
  • Robertson D, Moreo N. Review of Björnevik et al. — many open-access commentaries exist; pick one and critique it alongside the paper.

Session 6 — Molecular mimicry

Section titled “Session 6 — Molecular mimicry”

Anchor paper. Lanz TV et al. Clonally expanded B cells in multiple sclerosis bind EBV EBNA1 and GlialCAM. Nature 2022. doi:10.1038/s41586-022-04432-7

Why this matters. Pair this with session 5. Björnevik says EBV is necessary; Lanz offers a mechanism — antibodies against EBV EBNA1 cross-react with the CNS protein GlialCAM. This is the model under active refinement right now.

Discussion prompts.

  1. What experimental evidence would distinguish “molecular mimicry drives MS” from “molecular mimicry is one of several triggers”?
  2. The authors examined clonally expanded B cells in MS patients’ CSF. Why CSF rather than blood?
  3. How would you use this paper to design a prevention study?

MSKB concepts. EBV & MS · B-cell therapies

Supporting resources.

  • Jelcic I et al. Memory B cells activate brain-homing, autoreactive CD4+ T cells in multiple sclerosis. Cell 2018 — companion mechanistic paper.

Session 7 — Genetics at scale

Section titled “Session 7 — Genetics at scale”

Anchor paper. International Multiple Sclerosis Genetics Consortium. Multiple sclerosis genomic map implicates peripheral immune cells and microglia in susceptibility. Science 2019. doi:10.1126/science.aav7188

Why this matters. 47k cases, 68k controls, 233 susceptibility loci. The signal isn’t in one gene — it’s in the pattern of which cell types the risk variants are expressed in. This paper is how you teach “polygenic” in one evening.

Discussion prompts.

  1. What is a Manhattan plot, and what would a single-gene disease look like on one?
  2. Why does “the signal is in microglia and peripheral immune cells” matter for drug target selection?
  3. How does HLA-DRB1*15:01 compare, in effect size, to the other 200+ loci?

MSKB concepts. Genetic susceptibility

Supporting resources.

  • Hauser SL, Cree BAC. Treatment of multiple sclerosis: a review. Am J Med 2020 — ties genetics to modern DMT strategy.
  • GWAS catalog — ebi.ac.uk/gwas — pull up the MS study and practice reading the summary stats.

Session 8 — B-cell depletion

Section titled “Session 8 — B-cell depletion”

Anchor papers. Hauser SL et al. Ocrelizumab versus interferon beta-1a in relapsing multiple sclerosis (OPERA I and II). NEJM 2017. doi:10.1056/NEJMoa1601277 · Optional pair: Montalban X et al. Ocrelizumab versus placebo in primary progressive multiple sclerosis (ORATORIO). NEJM 2017. doi:10.1056/NEJMoa1606468

Why this matters. The trial that remade MS. Two identical Phase 3 RRMS trials plus the first positive PPMS trial. Anti-CD20 therapy is now first-line for many patients.

Discussion prompts.

  1. In OPERA, ocrelizumab was compared to interferon beta-1a, not placebo. Why, and how does that change the interpretation of the annualized relapse rate reduction?
  2. ORATORIO’s primary endpoint was 12-week confirmed disability progression. Why not 24 weeks? Read the supplement.
  3. What safety signal — present but under-discussed in the 2017 papers — has become more important with long-term use?

MSKB concepts. B-cell therapies · DMT overview

Supporting resources.

  • CONSORT checklist — equator-network.org/reporting-guidelines/consort/. Walk through OPERA alongside the checklist as a group exercise.

Session 9 — Progression without relapse (PIRA)

Section titled “Session 9 — Progression without relapse (PIRA)”

Anchor paper. Kappos L et al. Contribution of relapse-independent progression vs relapse-associated worsening to overall confirmed disability accumulation in typical relapsing multiple sclerosis in a pooled analysis of two randomized clinical trials. JAMA Neurol 2020. doi:10.1001/jamaneurol.2020.1568

Why this matters. The paper that gave PIRA (Progression Independent of Relapse Activity) a name and a measurement. It reframed what progressive MS is and changed the target of the next generation of drugs.

Discussion prompts.

  1. How do the authors operationalise “relapse-independent” progression? What time window separates relapse-associated from independent worsening?
  2. Roughly what fraction of confirmed disability worsening in the pooled OPERA cohort was PIRA? Does the answer surprise you for “relapsing” MS?
  3. If PIRA is real, what does that imply for when a patient should be switched to a high-efficacy DMT?

MSKB concepts. PIRA & smoldering MS · Disease course

Supporting resources.

  • Lublin FD et al. How patients with MS accumulate disability. Ann Neurol 2022 — an updated replication with additional cohorts.

Session 10 — Blood biomarkers

Section titled “Session 10 — Blood biomarkers”

Anchor paper. Disanto G et al. Serum neurofilament light: a biomarker of neuronal damage in multiple sclerosis. Ann Neurol 2017. doi:10.1002/ana.24954

Why this matters. A blood test that tracks neuroaxonal damage is the kind of thing that was science fiction in 2010. It is now clinically deployable. Read this to understand what “biomarker validation” actually looks like.

Discussion prompts.

  1. What is the difference between analytical validation and clinical validation of a biomarker? Which did this paper do?
  2. Serum NfL rises with age in healthy people. How should a clinician interpret a single NfL value in a 55-year-old MS patient?
  3. What would you need to see before using NfL as a treatment decision tool rather than a monitoring tool?

MSKB concepts. CSF & blood biomarkers

Supporting resources.

  • Benkert P et al. Serum neurofilament light chain for individual prognostication of disease activity in people with multiple sclerosis. Lancet Neurol 2022 — the reference-range paper.

Session 11 — The repair problem

Section titled “Session 11 — The repair problem”

Anchor paper. Franklin RJM, Ffrench-Constant C. Remyelination in the CNS: from biology to therapy. Nat Rev Neurosci 2008. doi:10.1038/nrn2480

Why this matters. Anti-inflammatory drugs stop new damage. They do not repair old damage. Remyelination biology is the other half of the MS problem, and it is still unsolved in 2026.

Discussion prompts.

  1. What is an oligodendrocyte precursor cell (OPC), and why is its failure to differentiate — rather than its absence — the central puzzle?
  2. The paper predates every failed remyelination trial (opicinumab, clemastine, high-dose biotin). Which predictions have held up, and which look wrong in hindsight?
  3. Design a Phase 2 remyelination trial. What is the primary endpoint? How small can you get the sample while still answering a meaningful question?

MSKB concepts. Demyelination & remyelination

Supporting resources.

  • Cunniffe N, Coles A. Promoting remyelination in multiple sclerosis. J Neurol 2021 — the modern status report.

Session 12 — Equity and the underserved majority

Section titled “Session 12 — Equity and the underserved majority”

Anchor paper. Amezcua L, McCauley JL. Race and ethnicity on MS presentation and disease course. Mult Scler J 2020. doi:10.1177/1352458519887328

Why this matters. MS was long labelled a “white person’s disease.” That framing is wrong and it has produced real harm — underdiagnosis, later treatment, worse outcomes in Black and Hispanic patients. A good journal club ends here, not as a footnote.

Discussion prompts.

  1. How do the authors disentangle biological differences from healthcare access and representation in trials differences? Where are they confident and where are they honest about uncertainty?
  2. Every clinical trial paper you read this semester had a demographics table. Go back to OPERA (session 8). What fraction of participants were Black? Hispanic? How does that compare to the U.S. MS population?
  3. If you were designing the next Phase 3 MS trial, what are three concrete changes you’d make to the enrolment plan?

MSKB concepts. Equity & social determinants · Pediatric MS

Supporting resources.

  • Langer-Gould A et al. Incidence of multiple sclerosis in multiple racial and ethnic groups. Neurology 2013.
  • NMSS Defeat MS Alliance — health equity program page at nationalmssociety.org.

Standing resources (useful every week)

Section titled “Standing resources (useful every week)”

Finding MS papers.

  • PubMed — the default; save a query like multiple sclerosis[MeSH] AND 2024:2026[dp].
  • OpenAlex — the citation graph MSKB is built on; good for finding forward citations.
  • ClinicalTrials.gov — every trial you read should be looked up here too.

Appraising MS papers.

  • CASP checklists — one per study design. Use them, do not skim them.
  • EQUATOR Network — reporting guidelines (CONSORT, STROBE, PRISMA, STARD, ARRIVE for animal work).
  • Greenhalgh T. How to read a paper. BMJ Books. Short; worth owning.

MS-specific context.

  • National MS Society — Research portal. Accessible summaries of current priorities and the Pathways to Cures roadmap.
  • MS International Federation — Atlas of MS. Prevalence and treatment-access data by country.
  • ECTRIMS and ACTRIMS. Annual meetings; abstract books are a fast way to find hot topics.

For the presenter.

  • Slide template: Question → Design → Result → Limitation → What next. Five slides, one per bullet. Resist adding more.
  • Before you present, read the paper twice. Second pass with CASP open beside you.

Next steps after this track

Students who finish this track are ready to sit in on a live lab meeting or attend ECTRIMS/ACTRIMS with context. Good follow-on pathways: Mechanistic for deeper biology, Emerging topics for open frontiers, Intro to MS research for study design depth.
