Emerging Topics & Research Frontiers

Why this page exists

The National MS Society’s Pathways to Cures roadmap (Bebo et al., 2022 & 2024) synthesized input from 300+ people living with MS, 30+ MS societies, and the global research community into a shared agenda. This page distills that agenda into the challenges and frontiers most likely to shape the next decade of MS research — and the questions that need the next generation of investigators.

The Pathways to Cures framework

Section titled “The Pathways to Cures framework”

The NMSS organizes its research agenda around three interdependent pillars. Progress in each pillar is necessary but not sufficient — ending MS forever requires all three.

PillarCore questionWhere the field is
Stop MSHow do we halt the underlying disease process, especially in progressive MS?Promising — BTK inhibitors in Phase 3, smoldering MS newly measurable
Restore functionCan we repair damage that has already occurred?Early-stage — no approved remyelinating therapy yet
End MS foreverCan we prevent MS from developing in the first place?Breakthrough moment — EBV causality now established, vaccines in trials

Pillar 1 — Stop MS

Section titled “Pillar 1 — Stop MS”

The challenge: smoldering disease behind the BBB

Section titled “The challenge: smoldering disease behind the BBB”

For decades, MS research focused on controlling relapses — the dramatic, visible attacks driven by peripheral immune cells breaching the blood-brain barrier. The field succeeded: modern anti-CD20 therapies suppress relapse rates by >50%. Yet disability keeps accumulating in many patients even when MRI shows no new lesions and relapses stop.

Why? The answer reshaping the field is smoldering neuroinflammation: a slow, chronic disease process driven by immune cells (microglia, B cells, T cells) already resident inside the CNS, behind the BBB, largely invisible to the therapies that work brilliantly in the periphery. This process drives:

  • PIRA (Progression Independent of Relapse Activity) — disability that worsens between attacks, newly measurable as a trial endpoint
  • Slowly Expanding Lesions (SELs) — old lesion borders that keep creeping outward for years
  • Paramagnetic Rim Lesions (PRLs) — chronic active lesions with a ferritic iron rim visible on 7T (and some 3T) MRI, a marker of ongoing smoldering inflammation

Why PIRA changes everything

PIRA reframes how we measure progressive MS. Instead of waiting years for EDSS milestones, researchers can now ask: does this therapy slow the rate of silent disability accumulation? This opens trials to drugs that work inside the CNS rather than in the periphery.

The frontier: CNS-penetrant therapies

Section titled “The frontier: CNS-penetrant therapies”

BTK inhibitors (Bruton’s tyrosine kinase inhibitors) are the most watched class of drugs in MS right now. Unlike anti-CD20 antibodies, which are large proteins excluded by the BBB, small-molecule BTK inhibitors penetrate the CNS and can reach resident B cells and microglia directly.

Three are in Phase 3 trials: tolebrutinib, fenebrutinib, and evobrutinib. The 2024 NMSS roadmap identifies CNS-penetrant immunotherapy as a top priority — the first real chance to reach smoldering inflammation where it lives.

Open questions for the next generation:

  • What distinguishes patients whose PIRA is driven by smoldering inflammation from those with pure neurodegeneration?
  • Can paramagnetic rim lesion count serve as a Phase 2 surrogate endpoint for smoldering disease?
  • Why does progressive MS develop in some RRMS patients and not others — and what predicts conversion?
PIRA & smoldering MS Disability that accumulates silently between relapses — the mechanism behind progressive MS.
BTK inhibitors CNS-penetrant small molecules targeting resident microglia and B cells.
Paramagnetic rim lesions Chronic active lesions as an imaging biomarker for smoldering inflammation.

Pillar 2 — Restore Function

Section titled “Pillar 2 — Restore Function”

The challenge: repair is possible but incomplete

Section titled “The challenge: repair is possible but incomplete”

The adult CNS retains a remarkable capacity for remyelination. After a demyelinating attack, oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs) migrate to lesion sites, differentiate, and rewrap naked axons. In early MS, this repair is often sufficient for clinical recovery after a relapse. But remyelination fails progressively over the course of the disease.

Why? OPCs are still present — even in chronic lesions, the cells needed for repair are there. Something blocks them from differentiating. The field has identified several candidate inhibitory signals:

  • Lingo-1 (an axonal signaling molecule that blocks OPC differentiation)
  • RGMa (repulsive guidance molecule A)
  • Senescent OPCs — cells that have stopped responding to pro-remyelination signals
  • Reactive astrocytes — glia that create a hostile environment for repair

And once axons degenerate — once the bare wire shorts and dies — remyelination cannot restore function. Neuroprotection (preventing axonal loss in the first place) is the other half of this problem.

The remyelination gap

Despite multiple Phase 2 trials over the past decade (opicinumab, clemastine, biotin), no remyelinating therapy has yet proven effective in a Phase 3 trial. This is one of the most important unmet needs in the field. It is also one of the most actively worked problems — the biology is now well enough understood to design better trials.

The frontier: repair biology meets measurable endpoints

Section titled “The frontier: repair biology meets measurable endpoints”

Progress in two directions is accelerating this pillar:

Biology: A clearer understanding of why remyelination fails in chronic lesions — OPC senescence, inhibitory signals, astrocytic scarring — is generating new therapeutic targets. Agents in trials include PIPE-307 (M1R muscarinic receptor antagonist) and novel anti-LINGO approaches.

Endpoints: You can’t run a remyelination trial without measuring remyelination. Magnetization transfer ratio (MTR), myelin water imaging, and visual evoked potential (VEP) latency are emerging as measurable surrogates for myelin content in lesions, allowing proof-of-concept trials in smaller cohorts.

Open questions for the next generation:

  • Can we identify which lesions are “remyelination-competent” vs. chronically irreparable at diagnosis?
  • Is there a therapeutic window after a lesion forms within which remyelination therapy must be given?
  • How do we distinguish remyelination from axonal sprouting in imaging endpoints?
  • Can neuroprotective agents (simvastatin, ibudilast) slow brain atrophy independent of anti-inflammatory effects?
Demyelination & remyelination What happens when myelin is lost and why repair fails in progressive MS.
PIRA & smoldering MS The biological and clinical overlap between progressive disease and repair failure.

Pillar 3 — End MS Forever

Section titled “Pillar 3 — End MS Forever”

The breakthrough: EBV causality established

Section titled “The breakthrough: EBV causality established”

In January 2022, a landmark study in Science by Björnevik et al. followed 10 million US military personnel for 20 years with serial blood samples. Among 955 who developed MS, every single one had been infected with Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) before disease onset — in contrast to a control group where EBV infection lagged behind. The risk of MS increased 32-fold after EBV infection and not at all before it.

This is the clearest evidence yet of a necessary environmental cause of MS — one without which MS essentially does not develop.

Later that year, Lanz et al. in Nature provided a mechanistic explanation: EBV protein EBNA1 structurally mimics GlialCAM, a protein expressed by astrocytes and oligodendrocytes. Antibodies generated against EBNA1 during infection can cross-react with GlialCAM in the CNS — a molecular mimicry mechanism that could explain how EBV primes the autoimmune response decades before symptoms appear.

A rare moment in medicine

Establishing a necessary environmental cause of a common autoimmune disease — with a target for prevention — is exceptionally rare. The EBV-MS connection now opens a path that didn’t exist five years ago: preventing MS by preventing EBV infection.

The frontier: vaccination and preclinical MS

Section titled “The frontier: vaccination and preclinical MS”

EBV vaccines are in active clinical development for the first time in history, with MS prevention as a downstream goal. ModernaTX’s mRNA-1189 EBV vaccine and GSK’s gp350-adjuvanted vaccine are among those in early trials. Timeline to MS-specific efficacy data is likely 10–15 years, but the scientific case is compelling enough to justify the investment.

Complementing vaccination is the concept of preclinical MS: using blood biomarkers (NfL, specific antibody patterns, genetic risk scores) to identify individuals at elevated risk years before their first lesion. If we can identify preclinical MS and intervene early, we may prevent the first demyelinating event entirely.

Open questions for the next generation:

  • Which EBV proteins are most important for molecular mimicry — EBNA1 alone, or a broader repertoire?
  • What additional factors determine who develops MS after EBV infection (genetics, timing, immune state)?
  • What would a preclinical MS screening program look like, and what are its ethical implications?
  • Can EBV vaccine trials use MS incidence as a primary endpoint, and what is the required sample size?
EBV and molecular mimicry The epidemiological and mechanistic evidence linking EBV infection to MS onset.
Genetic susceptibility HLA-DRB1, non-HLA loci, and gene-environment interactions in MS risk.
Environmental risk factors EBV, vitamin D, smoking — how environment interacts with genetics to trigger MS.

Cross-cutting frontiers

Section titled “Cross-cutting frontiers”

Biomarkers for the whole arc of disease

Section titled “Biomarkers for the whole arc of disease”

Blood-based biomarkers have transformed what’s measurable outside the MRI scanner:

  • Serum NfL (neurofilament light chain) — rises during relapses, correlates with brain atrophy, predicts long-term disability. The first clinically deployable blood biomarker for MS disease activity.
  • Serum GFAP (glial fibrillary acidic protein) — elevated in progressive MS even without relapses; may reflect smoldering astrocyte activation.
  • Paramagnetic rim lesion count — the leading imaging biomarker for chronic active inflammation; associated with faster progression and worse outcomes.

Together, these create the possibility of precision monitoring: following patients between clinic visits, catching silent worsening early, and using biomarkers as trial endpoints.

Artificial intelligence and the digital clinic

Section titled “Artificial intelligence and the digital clinic”

The same lesion on MRI can be interpreted differently by two radiologists. AI-based automated lesion segmentation is approaching radiologist-level accuracy and can detect subtle changes invisible to the human eye. Beyond imaging, AI is being applied to:

  • Predicting which patients will progress to SPMS
  • Identifying treatment non-responders before disability accumulates
  • Integrating genomic, imaging, and clinical data into richer prognostic models

The NMSS roadmap identifies AI as a cross-cutting infrastructure need — not a standalone research priority, but the substrate that enables all three pillars to move faster.

Equity: the underserved majority

Section titled “Equity: the underserved majority”

MS affects Black Americans at higher rates than previously recognized and with more aggressive disease course on average — yet Black patients are underrepresented in clinical trials, underdiagnosed due to the mistaken assumption that MS is a “white person’s disease,” and undertreated relative to white patients.

Hispanic populations are the fastest-growing demographic in the United States and among the least studied in MS research. The global MS burden is also shifting — with rising incidence documented in regions (Iran, South America) historically considered low-prevalence.

The NMSS roadmap treats equity as a core pillar, not an afterthought. The next generation of MS researchers needs to look like the population affected by the disease.

CSF & blood biomarkers NfL, GFAP, and oligoclonal bands — blood-based markers for disease activity and progression.
Equity & social determinants Racial/ethnic disparities in MS incidence, diagnosis, and treatment access.

What can you contribute?

Section titled “What can you contribute?”

The NMSS Pathways to Cures roadmap is explicit about where more investigators are needed:

High-priority gaps the field acknowledges

  1. Progressive MS biology — We understand relapsing MS far better than progressive MS. The biological drivers of slow, smoldering worsening are incompletely known.

  2. Remyelination endpoints — Better imaging and biomarker tools to measure myelin repair would unlock faster proof-of-concept trials.

  3. EBV mechanism — The molecular mimicry hypothesis explains some MS, but the full immunological story of how EBV primes MS-specific autoimmunity is not yet written.

  4. Equity-focused cohorts — Prospective studies that deliberately enroll diverse populations are needed to understand why MS presents differently across racial and ethnic groups.

  5. Patient-centered outcomes — Fatigue, cognition, and quality of life — the domains patients rank as most important — are still poorly served by existing trial endpoints.

  6. Prevention trial infrastructure — Running EBV vaccine trials with MS as an endpoint requires registries, biobank infrastructure, and long follow-up that doesn’t yet exist at scale.

The gap between what we know and what we need to know is large — and that is the opportunity.
